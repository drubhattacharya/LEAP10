This is a data architecture failure, not a technology failure.

With those three fields added to the EHR for 90 days, we can run this test again with the right instrument. In the meantime, both high- and low-tech units have a median delay of 24–26 minutes — nearly 2.5 times the recommended standard. The investment may eventually prove itself, but the data we have today shows no patient-level benefit yet."

That gap is where diagnostic errors, medication errors, and non-adherence are born.
    Nuanced Insight
    The medical director's framing ("documentation burden") mistakes the symptom for the disease. The issue is not that documentation is hard — it is that teach-back is not part of the clinical workflow at all. "Teach-back performed" is only 8% — meaning 92% of encounters skip the step entirely, not just the charting of it. Teach-back is the primary evidence-based method for doing this. At 8% performance and 1.7% documentation, this organization cannot demonstrate compliance with either standard in an audit. The director's argument — that this is a documentation burden — inadvertently creates a far larger burden: malpractice liability when a patient cannot recall their discharge instructions and readmits, or suffers a preventable complication.

    Two-step clinical redesign: (1) Embed teach-back in the existing workflow — not as an added step, but as a modification of the current discharge instruction process: replace "Did you explain the instructions?" (Yes/No) with "Patient was able to correctly repeat back: medication name, timing, and one warning sign" (Yes/No/Attempted). The click count is identical; the clinical information is fundamentally different. (2) Decouple the 5 stages into two accountability streams: Patient Experience metrics ("felt respected," "provider listened") should go to the PX dashboard. Comprehension-verification metrics ("teach-back performed and documented") should go to the clinical quality and compliance dashboard — where they belong alongside medication reconciliation and fall-risk assessment. This reframing moves teach-back out of the "patient satisfaction" bucket and into the "patient safety" bucket — which is where the director will act.

    Your Interpretation
    The risk manager says "we ran the test and it's not significant — problem solved." Write a rebuttal memo in 4 sentences.
    Obvious Takeaway
    A null result from an underpowered test is not evidence of no effect — it is evidence that this test cannot detect the effect. With only 43 adverse events across 1,000 encounters, the chi-square has approximately 8% statistical power. We would expect to miss a real effect 92% of the time.
    Nuanced Insight
    There is also a directional confounding problem: the group with interpreter use (4.4% adverse events) appears slightly worse than the no-interpreter group (3.8%). This is almost certainly a severity confound — sicker patients trigger both interpreter requests and adverse events. The group that needed an interpreter and didn't get one (n=210) is likely a more heterogeneous group whose lower event rate reflects case mix, not protection. What we actually need is the inverse test: among patients with adverse events, what proportion involved documented language barriers and absent interpreters? That is the meaningful safety question — and it requires language context in the safety report, which currently 74.4% of adverse events lack.
    4-Sentence Rebuttal Memo
    "The chi-square test we ran has only ~8% statistical power given 43 adverse events in 1,000 encounters — meaning we would miss a real safety signal 9 times out of 10 with this sample. A non-significant result from an underpowered test is not evidence of safety; it is evidence that our dataset is too small for this analysis. More critically, only 25.6% of our adverse events have a safety report filed, and none of those reports currently capture language context — so we are measuring interpreter use vs. adverse events without knowing whether language was a contributing factor in any specific case. The appropriate remediation is to add a 'language barrier present' field to all adverse event reports and conduct a prospective analysis over 12 months, targeting a sample size of 3,000 encounters to achieve 80% statistical power."

    Exercise 6.3 · Statistical Power Analysis · Risk Management Capstone
    QBS Qualification and Safety Events: When the Data Cannot Speak — and What to Do About It
    LAI Domain: Quality & Safety · Workforce Development · KPI 2: Adverse event rate by QBS qualification · Analysis: Statistical power and sample size — why the test that matters most cannot currently be run · Risk Management implication: data architecture as a patient safety infrastructure
    1
    Clinical Context
    This exercise is unlike the others. You are not going to find a clean, significant result — because the data cannot deliver one yet. And that is exactly the lesson.

    The most clinically important hypothesis in this entire dataset is: Are encounters with QBS-qualified providers safer? QBS (Qualified Bilingual Staff) credentialing is the highest-investment, highest-complexity workforce intervention in language access. If QBS qualification reduces adverse events, safety events, or avoidable LOS, the business case for the credential is overwhelming. If it does not, the investment needs to be reconsidered.

    Here is what the data actually shows:
    QBS-qualified encounters: n = 68 (6.8% of 1,000)
    Safety events in QBS encounters: 0
    Safety events in non-QBS encounters: 11 / 932 (1.18%)
    Adverse events in QBS encounters: 4 / 68 (5.9%)
    Adverse events in non-QBS encounters: 39 / 932 (4.2%)
    A chi-square test on these counts would produce unreliable results because QBS encounters are too few and events too rare. The expected cell counts fall below 5 — violating the chi-square assumption. Fisher's exact test could technically run, but with n=68 QBS encounters and 0 safety events in that group, the result would be uninterpretable as evidence of QBS effectiveness.

    The exercise: You will work through a power analysis to determine what sample size is actually needed to test this hypothesis — and design the data infrastructure required to build that sample.

    4
    Your Interpretation
    Your Patient Safety Officer asks: "Does the 0 safety events in 68 QBS encounters prove that QBS works?" A board member asks: "The adverse event rate in QBS encounters (5.9%) is actually higher than non-QBS (4.2%) — should we stop QBS investment?" How do you address both misinterpretations — and what is the risk management infrastructure you need to build to answer these questions properly in 18 months?
    Obvious Takeaway
    Zero safety events in 68 QBS encounters does not prove QBS works — it proves only that zero events happened in a very small sample, which is what you would expect when the base rate is 1.2% and you have only 68 observations (expected events = 0.8). The PSO's interpretation is a classic "absence of evidence" error. Separately, the 5.9% adverse event rate in QBS encounters vs. 4.2% in non-QBS encounters almost certainly reflects confounding: QBS providers are likely deployed to the most complex, highest-risk encounters — which would naturally have higher adverse event rates regardless of who provides language access.
    Nuanced Insight
    The board member's interpretation is the more dangerous one, because it could lead to defunding a safety intervention based on a statistical artifact. The higher adverse event rate in QBS encounters is almost certainly a severity confounding effect: QBS staff are called in for the most linguistically and clinically complex cases — obstetric emergencies, behavioral health crises, high-acuity inpatient admissions. Those encounters have higher adverse event rates for reasons entirely unrelated to QBS qualification. To compare QBS fairly against non-QBS, you would need to control for case complexity, service line, and diagnosis severity — none of which is possible in this dataset with n=68. The most important data architecture finding is this: you currently have no way to measure the ROI or safety effect of your most expensive language access workforce investment. That is a strategic risk.
    18-Month Data Infrastructure Plan
    Three fields to add immediately to every encounter: (1) qbs_provider_id — link QBS qualification to provider ID at the encounter level (not just patient encounter), enabling provider-level tracking; (2) encounter_acuity_score — a 1–5 clinical acuity rating at time of triage (already in most EHRs as triage level or ESI score) to enable severity adjustment; (3) language_access_modality_at_each_touchpoint — which modality was used at each of the 7 touchpoints (QBS, VRI, in-person interpreter, phone, untrained bilingual staff, none).

    Power calculation milestone: With expanded QBS coverage targeting 20% of encounters (200/1,000), and data collection across 3 sites for 12 months (~3,000 QBS encounters), adequate power is achievable within 18 months for a severity-adjusted analysis.

    To the PSO and the board member: "Our data cannot currently answer the QBS safety question — and that is the finding. We know QBS is required by CLAS standards, we know it is the standard of care for complex LEP encounters, and we know we cannot yet measure its safety effect. Our 18-month plan closes that measurement gap. In the meantime, we will not defund an evidence-based intervention because we lack the data to measure it — we will build the data infrastructure to measure it."
