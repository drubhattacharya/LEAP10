MODULE 1 · Pre-Assessment & Triage Communication
ED Language Access & Triage
Timely language access at triage (≤10 min) reduces three measurable financial exposures: (1) communication-driven over-admission, (2) same-complaint ED return visits, and (3) stroke treatment misclassification from onset-time reporting failure. Tracks 1 and 2 apply across all LEP ED encounters; the Stroke sub-model is the most precisely quantifiable single-pathway example of the same mechanism.
Track 1 — Admission Avoidance (All LEP ED Encounters)
Evidence basis: Schulson et al. (2018, JGIM, n=57,435 ED visits) found LEP patients were significantly more likely to be admitted (OR 1.09) and to receive additional imaging — with cardiovascular LEP patients facing OR 1.51 for stress testing. Mechanism: when clinicians cannot obtain a complete history, they compensate with more tests and higher admission rates to manage diagnostic uncertainty. Early language access removes that uncertainty at source. Default 9% excess admission rate maps directly to the OR 1.09 finding. Attribution to language access is conservative at 60% — acknowledging that LEP patients also have higher acuity and SDOH complexity.
All LEP ED encounters regardless of chief complaint. Distinct from the stroke-specific volume in the sub-model below.
Schulson (2018): OR 1.09 for admission. Default 9% is the marginal excess, not total LEP admission rate.
Proportion of excess admissions preventable through timely language access. Conservative default: 40%. Grounded in the finding that early interpreter use at admission (vs. only at discharge) reduces LOS 0.75–1.47 days (Lindholm 2012).
AHRQ HCUP median all-cause inpatient stay ~$11,000–$13,000. Use your organization’s direct cost per admission for precision.
Conservative: acknowledges that LEP patient admission rates also reflect higher acuity and SDOH complexity independent of communication barriers.
Track 2 — Same-Complaint Return Visit Avoidance
Evidence basis: A QI intervention study (PMC8677944) found that 79.2% of LEP 48-hour ED return visits were for the same complaint — the direct clinical signature of a first encounter where communication was insufficient to resolve the presenting problem. Standardizing LEP identification and interpreter use documentation reduced return visit rates measurably. Unlike language-concordance in the inpatient setting (where bilingual presence improves throughput), the ED return visit mechanism is purely about completeness: when a patient leaves without understanding their diagnosis or discharge instructions, they come back. This track captures the cost of that cycle.
LEP patients discharged from ED (total minus admissions). Default 170 = 200 total − ~30 admitted.
Marginal excess return rate attributable to communication gaps. Conservative default: 3%. Literature supports LEP patients have higher return visit rates; 79.2% of those returns are for the same complaint.
Proportion of excess returns preventable through systematic interpreter identification + documentation. QI evidence supports 40–50% range with process standardization.
ED facility direct cost per visit. Range $900–$1,500 depending on acuity mix. Does not include potential admission cost if return visit escalates.
Conservative: same-complaint returns also reflect SDOH factors (no PCP, medication access). 55% is defensible given the 79.2% same-complaint rate in LEP return visits.

MODULE 3 · Inpatient Communication Reliability
Engagement & LOS Reduction
Systematic communication integration during inpatient rounding reduces avoidable LOS. This module has two components: a baseline saving from interpreter integration into care delivery, and an incremental QBS Enhancement saving from increasing Qualified Bilingual Speaker coverage of LEP encounters. The QBS uplift is modeled as additive and calibrated to a diabetes-specific mechanism chain (Karliner et al., 2007; Lindholm et al., 2012).
Baseline — Interpreter Integration
Lindholm (2012): 1.5–3.0 day LEP premium overall. Default 1.0 day assumes systematic integration — not just interpreter availability. Ex. 3.2 null result confirms availability alone is insufficient.
QBS Enhancement — Bilingual Provider Uplift
Evidence basis: Karliner et al. (2007) found QBS providers produce fewer clinical errors and higher comprehension scores than ad hoc staff, outperforming professional interpreters on several quality dimensions. The mechanism: faster complete HPI, real-time lab explanation, bedside injection-fear resolution, and same-visit discharge confirmation — each eliminating a deferred-communication loop that extends inpatient stays. Within Lindholm’s 1.5–3.0 day LEP premium, a QBS-specific contribution of 0.5–1.5 days is defensible for high-complexity encounters (new insulin initiation, comorbid CKD). Default: 0.5 days (conservative end).
% of LEP inpatient encounters that currently involve a QBS provider at any care stage. Default 20% typical for organizations with some bilingual clinical staff.
Realistic 12-month target. The incremental saving applies only to the coverage delta (target − current).
Additional LOS reduction attributable specifically to QBS presence vs. interpreter-only. Conservative default: 0.5 days. For high-complexity diabetes/CKD admissions: 1.0–1.5 days is defensible.

MODULE 5 · Patient Safety & Risk Management
Malpractice Cost-Avoidance
Language barrier claims represent ~2.5% of malpractice filings but up to 14% of high-severity claims (CRICO Strategies, 2015). This module uses a two-track model calibrated to the UC Berkeley / NHeLP claims analysis (Quan & Lynch, 2010), inflation-adjusted to 2025, separating damage exposure from defense legal cost exposure — which arises even when claims are dismissed. Replace defaults with your organization’s actual risk data for the most defensible projection.
Encounter Profile
Berkeley: documentation deficiencies present in virtually all language-barrier claims reviewed. Range: 20–65%.
Claim Economics — Berkeley Calibrated
Berkeley: 35 language-barrier claims / 1,373 total reviewed = 2.55%. Under-filing is probable; true incidence exceeds filed rate.
Berkeley: 10 of 35 claims (28.6%) paid damages. Remaining 91% still incurred defense legal costs regardless of outcome.
Cost Decomposition — Inflation-Adjusted to 2025
Berkeley avg $228,900 (2010) × CPI adj. = $336K. PIAA/Candello 2022–2024 avg: $350–500K. CA MICRA cap rising to $750K by 2033 (AB 35).
Berkeley avg $79,800 (2010) × CPI adj. = $117K. Applies even to dismissed claims. Observed range: $6K–$588K per case.
Risk Reduction — Two Mechanisms
Reduces adverse event incidence and claim filing rate. Systematic protocol studies: 10–30% range.
Reduces claim success rate and time-to-dismissal. Applied multiplicatively with interpreter reduction. Berkeley: no case had complete documentation.
Berkeley / NHeLP Key Findings (Quan & Lynch 2010, adjusted to 2025 USD):  35 language-barrier claims generated $3.36M in damages and $4.10M in defense legal fees — $7.46M combined, representing 2.5% of reviewed claims. Only 29% resulted in damage payment; 91% incurred defense legal costs regardless of outcome. Dominant failure modes: interpreter absence (32/35 • 91%), defective informed consent (12/35 • 34%), documentation gaps (virtually all cases), discrimination allegation (2/35 • uncapped under CA Unruh Act). Post-2010: CRICO Strategies data shows language-barrier claims generate approximately 7× the severity concentration of general malpractice claims.
