Foundation 1 of 5 - Design Principle
Language access failures do not end at discharge. They generate a second wave of harm — adverse events, safety incidents, and malpractice claims — that concentrates in the months after the encounter ends.

Module 5 addresses what happens when the communication failures documented in Modules 1 through 4 are not intercepted. The post-discharge period is when unresolved comprehension failures manifest as adverse outcomes, when safety events that occurred during hospitalization get reported — or, more commonly, do not get reported — and when malpractice exposure crystallizes into claims.

The Underreporting Gap
Exercise 6.1 finds that 74.4% of LEP adverse events are not reported through standard safety reporting channels. This is not a finding about event frequency — it is a finding about system blindness. An organization cannot manage risk it cannot see. The safety reporting infrastructure was designed for English-proficient patients and relies on self-report and staff identification of events in clinical workflows that are already failing for LEP patients.

Foundation 2 of 5 - The Data Gap
The 40x gap between what safety data shows and what incident reports capture is not measurement error. It is a governance failure.

Exercise 6.2 tests the association between safety event severity and language barrier documentation. The result is significant. When severity-stratified, the gap between administrative data and incident reports is approximately 40-fold for LEP patients: administrative records show 40 times more safety-relevant events than formal incident reports capture.

What the 40x Gap Means for Governance
A risk committee reviewing incident reports for LEP patients is reviewing approximately 2.5% of the actual safety event volume. The risk posture is being set based on an almost entirely invisible dataset. Every governance decision — staffing, protocol design, technology investment, malpractice reserve — is calibrated to a floor that does not exist. The 40x gap is not a data collection improvement opportunity. It is a fundamental governance visibility problem.

The mechanism is specific. Standard incident reports require staff to identify an event, classify it, and submit it through a workflow. For LEP patient events, the identification step fails most often: the communication failure that caused the event is not legible to the staff member as a reportable safety event because it presented as a clinical outcome, not as an identifiable process failure. The event happened; it was not recognized as language-barrier-attributable; it was not reported.[2]

Foundation 3 of 5 - Two-Track Malpractice Exposure
LEP malpractice exposure operates on two tracks simultaneously. Conflating them produces the wrong defense strategy.

Track 1 is clinical negligence: a communication failure caused or contributed to a clinical error that harmed the patient. The standard elements apply — duty, breach, causation, damages — with the additional complexity that the communication failure must be linked to the clinical outcome. Track 2 is civil rights liability under Section 1557: the organization failed to provide meaningful access to a LEP patient, regardless of clinical outcome. Track 2 liability exists even when no clinical harm occurred.

The Berkeley Evidence Base
The Berkeley Law / NHeLP (2010) analysis of 35 language access malpractice claims found: average defense cost $118,000; 91% of organizations incurred defense costs even when cases were resolved favorably; and Track 2 claims (civil rights) were often filed in parallel with Track 1 claims, doubling the legal exposure per incident. An organization that resolves the clinical negligence claim may still face a civil rights enforcement action for the same underlying event.

The governance implication of the two-track model is structural: the malpractice reserve and the compliance exposure must be managed separately, because their mitigation strategies are different. Track 1 is mitigated by improving clinical communication — qualified interpreters, QBS, teach-back documentation. Track 2 is mitigated by compliance infrastructure — T1-T7 touchpoint documentation, interpreter record linkage, preferred-language attestation in consent. An organization that invests only in Track 1 mitigation remains exposed on Track 2, and vice versa.[3]

Foundation 4 of 5 - Closing the Visibility Gap
You cannot reduce what you cannot see. Closing the 74.4% underreporting gap is the precondition for any post-discharge safety intervention.
1
Passive reporting infrastructure fails LEP patients structurally
Standard incident reporting depends on staff recognition of language-barrier-attributable events. When staff do not recognize communication failures as reportable safety events — because they present as clinical outcomes — passive reporting produces a systematically incomplete picture. The 74.4% underreporting rate is the direct consequence of a reporting system designed without LEP patient safety events in mind.
2
Active surveillance bridges the gap
Active surveillance — structured review of administrative data, readmission records, and post-discharge call data filtered by preferred language — identifies safety-relevant events without relying on staff recognition and voluntary reporting. This is not a technology solution; it is a workflow change that requires designating LEP safety event identification as a specific quality function, not a secondary consequence of general incident reporting.
3
The governance minimum: stratified safety data
The minimum governance standard is safety event data stratified by preferred language, reviewed quarterly at the same committee level that reviews overall patient safety performance. If the risk committee cannot answer the question — what is our LEP adverse event rate and how does it compare to our English-proficient patient rate — the governance infrastructure is operating on incomplete data.

An LEP patient experiences a medication error during hospitalization caused by an incomplete allergy history — which the patient provided in Spanish but was documented incompletely by an untested bilingual aide. Which malpractice track or tracks apply?
Correct. The medication error creates Track 1 exposure (clinical negligence — communication failure contributed to a clinical harm). The use of an unqualified interpreter for clinical history-taking creates Track 2 exposure (Section 1557 — meaningful access was not provided). Both exposures are independent and can be pursued simultaneously.
Track 1 applies, but Track 2 also applies independently. Using an unqualified interpreter for clinical communication is a Section 1557 violation regardless of whether harm occurred.
Track 1 applies because the communication failure caused a clinical harm. Track 2 applies because an unqualified interpreter was used. Both run simultaneously.
A general consent form does not waive Section 1557 rights or excuse clinical negligence caused by communication failure. Informed consent in the preferred language is a distinct requirement from general consent.
