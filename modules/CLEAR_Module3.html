Foundation 1 of 5 - Design Principle
Rework is not random. It concentrates exactly where communication failures concentrate — and it compounds across the stay.

Attendant tasks are the downstream tasks generated when clinical work is not completed correctly the first time. Coder queries because the history was incomplete. Physician callbacks because the medication reconciliation contained errors. Documentation addenda because the initial note did not reflect what the patient actually said. Each of these is a communication failure made visible as an operational cost.

The Rework Concentration Signal
Exercise 3.1 finds OB at 13.0 attendant tasks per encounter — the highest in the dataset and the largest H-statistic of any exercise. OB is also the service line with the longest interpreter delay (Module 1). The pattern is not coincidental. The same structural workflow failure that produces delay at triage produces rework at rounds. The mechanism is consistent; only the measurement changes.

The practical implication is that training-based interventions will not close the rework gap. Training communicates knowledge about what should happen. It does not change the workflow trigger that determines whether it does happen. Structural EHR prompts, hard-stops, and required fields at the point-of-care are the only interventions that reliably reduce rework at the rounding touchpoint.[1]

Foundation 2 of 5 - LOS Evidence
Length of stay is the financial face of rework. Every communication failure at rounds is a potential extra inpatient day.

Exercise 3.2 tests whether interpreter use at the inpatient rounding touchpoint is associated with shorter length of stay. The Mann-Whitney U result is non-significant (p = 0.172) but directionally important: the effect is in the expected direction with an 11.9-hour difference. The test is underpowered given the available sample — this is a near-miss, not a true null.

The Lindholm Anchor
Lindholm et al. (2012) established through a retrospective cohort study that LEP patients without professional interpretation at admission and discharge had 0.75 to 1.47 additional inpatient days. This is the financial model default. The ROI Dashboard uses 1.0 day as the conservative midpoint. The exercise result is directionally consistent with this evidence; the sample dataset is simply too small to confirm it independently.

The two-component LOS model prices the inpatient communication improvement opportunity. Component 1 is the baseline: systematic interpreter integration at admission and discharge touchpoints, applied to all LEP inpatient encounters. Component 2 is the QBS uplift: for advanced maternal, behavioral health, and high-acuity encounters, QBS-qualified providers can deliver an additional 0.5-day reduction by eliminating handoff delays and rework cycles that persist even when remote interpretation is available.[2]

Foundation 3 of 5 - The Training Trap
Training is not the intervention. Training is the announcement that an intervention is expected.

Exercise 3.3 finds that CLAS training currency is not significantly associated with reduced rework burden (p = 0.382). This result is consistent with Exercise 1.3 (technology without workflow) and Exercise 2.1 (documentation without delivery). The pattern across three modules is now clear: investments that operate at the awareness or documentation layer do not produce outcomes at the execution layer without a structural workflow change.

Why Training Fails to Move Operational Metrics
Clinical staff with CLAS training know that they should request an interpreter at rounds. When time pressure, staffing shortages, or workflow friction make that request inconvenient, training-based knowledge does not override the practical constraint. A hard-stop in the rounding note — requiring interpreter confirmation before the clinical note can be signed — converts the intervention from a voluntary behavior change into a required workflow step. These are categorically different interventions producing categorically different outcomes.

The governance implication is specific: before approving any language access training initiative, ask what happens when the trained behavior is inconvenient. If the answer is that the trained behavior will be skipped, the training is insufficient as the sole intervention. Identify the structural prompt that makes the behavior required regardless of convenience, and invest in that prompt alongside the training.[3]

Foundation 4 of 5 - The QBS Standard
A bilingual employee is not a qualified bilingual provider. The distinction is the difference between communication and assessed clinical communication competency.

Qualified Bilingual Staff (QBS) are providers and staff whose bilingual clinical language proficiency has been assessed using a validated instrument. QBS are not interpreters and are not interchangeable with remote interpretation. They are providers who can conduct clinical encounters in the patient preferred language without requiring a third party to mediate the exchange. This changes the communication dynamic in ways that neither remote interpretation nor untested bilingual staff use can replicate.

1
The Amobi (2024) Standard
Amobi et al. (2024, JAMA): "Would you bet someone's life on it?" as the QBS deployment standard. Bilingual staff who have not been assessed may have conversational fluency without clinical vocabulary, or clinical vocabulary in one specialty without fluency across the range of encounters they are expected to conduct. The JAMA framing establishes the standard: if the assessment has not been completed, the clinical communication competency cannot be assumed.
2
Dataset benchmark: 6.8% QBS coverage
The LAI Q1 2026 sample dataset shows 6.8% of providers with QBS qualification. The LAI KPI 2 scoring band reaches 100/100 at 20% QBS coverage — indicating that 6.8% places most organizations in the lowest performance band. The investment case for QBS enrollment is precisely the gap between current coverage and the level required to serve LEP patient volume without systematic delay.
3
Priority service lines for QBS investment
OB, Behavioral Health, and high-acuity inpatient units produce the highest rework burden and the longest interpreter delay. These are the priority service lines for QBS enrollment — not because QBS should replace interpretation in other settings, but because these settings produce the most clinical and financial benefit from same-language provider encounters at the rounding touchpoint specifically.

Formative Questions
Exercise 3.3 finds no significant association between CLAS training currency and rework reduction. What is the most defensible governance conclusion?
Correct. Training and structural workflow changes are complementary, not interchangeable. The null result tells you training alone is insufficient; it does not tell you training is worthless.
Cancellation misreads the finding. CLAS training has value as a knowledge foundation and a compliance requirement. The null result indicates it is insufficient as a standalone intervention, not that it should be eliminated.
The p-value of 0.382 is not near the significance threshold. Additional investment in training would not change the mechanism — training changes knowledge, not workflow execution.
QBS credentialing serves a different function from CLAS training. QBS qualifies providers for language-concordant clinical care. CLAS training addresses all staff awareness of language access obligations. Both serve distinct purposes.
The Lindholm et al. (2012) finding is that LEP patients without interpretation had 0.75 to 1.47 additional inpatient days. The ROI model defaults to 1.0 day. Why is the conservative midpoint the correct default?
Correct. The credibility of the financial case depends on the assumptions holding under scrutiny. A CFO who challenges the 1.47-day assumption and finds you used the upper bound will discount the entire analysis. A CFO who challenges the 1.0-day assumption and finds you used the conservative midpoint will increase confidence in the remaining figures.
A larger ROI figure that cannot be defended under scrutiny actively damages the credibility of the financial case. Finance committees remember when projections are challenged and collapse.
No federal guideline specifies the LOS reduction figure for language access ROI modeling. The choice of conservative anchor is a governance decision, not a regulatory requirement.
Simplicity of calculation is not a basis for evidence selection in a financial model that will be presented to a board finance committee.
