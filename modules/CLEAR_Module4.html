Foundation 1 of 5 - Design Principle
98.3% of LEP encounters reach discharge without verified comprehension. That is not a gap. That is the baseline.

The gap analysis in Exercise 4.2 is the most operationally concentrated finding in the module series. Teach-back performed: 3.3%. Teach-back documented: 1.7%. Neither performed nor documented: 98.3%. High patient satisfaction scores coexist with near-zero verified comprehension at the transition point with the highest clinical and financial consequence.

Why 98.3% Is Actionable
A 98.3% gap does not mean 98.3% of clinicians are failing. It means 98.3% of encounters do not have a workflow trigger that requires verified comprehension before discharge proceeds. This is a system design finding. The correct intervention is a non-bypassable EHR field at the discharge step, not a staff accountability initiative. When the workflow requires the action, the rate changes.

Hong et al. (2019) demonstrated HR 0.77 in condition-related 30-day readmission with teach-back methodology — a 23% lower rate. The financial model prices this as a 12-month trajectory: provider adoption grows linearly while automation (EHR-embedded prompts, automated post-discharge calls) follows an S-curve. Foundation 2 of 5 - The Dual Adoption Model
Provider adoption and automation adoption follow different curves. Understanding both is required for a credible 12-month projection.

Provider adoption of teach-back grows linearly over 12 months, limited by clinical behavior change timelines, training cycles, and workflow integration. A provider who is newly trained in teach-back methodology reaches 50% adoption by month 6 and approaches peak adoption by month 12 under normal implementation conditions.

Automation adoption follows an S-curve. The first 90 days show slow uptake as EHR fields are deployed and staff adjust to the workflow. Months 3 through 9 show rapid acceleration as the trigger becomes embedded in routine discharge processes. Months 9 through 12 approach capacity plateau. Net savings from automation exceed provider-only implementation in the back half of the year — making the automation infrastructure investment the higher-priority financial decision.

The Malevanchik Connection
Malevanchik et al. (2021) found that even with an active care transitions program reaching patients post-discharge, LEP patients reported significantly more difficulty with all measured domains: understanding discharge instructions (11.3% vs 6.5%), obtaining prescriptions (8.3% vs 5.5%), and managing medications. The program reached them — but it could not compensate for comprehension failures that occurred during the inpatient stay. Prevention at discharge is fundamentally more efficient than post-discharge compensation.
Foundation 3 of 5 - Billing Defensibility
The same documentation failure that drives readmissions also undermines billing defensibility. The CFO and CMO are looking at the same gap.

Exercise 4.3 tests whether avoidable LOS and missed reimbursement are correlated. The result is non-significant (p = 0.490). The interpretation: both are uniformly high regardless of patient subgroup. This is not a risk-stratification problem. It is a systemic billing documentation failure distributed across all discharge encounters.[2]

Three billing risks concentrate at the discharge touchpoint. First, avoidable LOS days that are not coded as communication-attributable — leaving the organization with the cost and without the clinical context that would support an appeal. Second, missed T1013 interpreter service billing in states with Medicaid reimbursement — a recoverable revenue stream that requires interpreter documentation linked to specific encounter events. Third, teach-back documentation gaps that undermine audit defensibility for patient education coding in post-acute billing claims.

Foundation 4 of 5 - Concordance Is Necessary But Not Sufficient
Language-concordant discharge instructions are necessary. They are not the same as verified comprehension.

Exercise 4.1 tests whether language-concordant discharge is associated with lower 30-day readmission. The result is non-significant (p = 0.782). This is a confounding result, not a clinical finding. Patients receiving concordant discharge instructions differ systematically from those who do not — by case complexity, by care team, and by service line — making cross-sectional comparison unreliable.

The Concordance-to-Comprehension Distinction
A discharge instruction document in the preferred language increases the probability that the patient can read it. It does not verify that they understood it, can recall it, or can execute its instructions under the conditions they will face at home. Concordance addresses information access. Teach-back verifies information processing. Both are necessary; neither substitutes for the other. The null result here tells you that concordance alone, without teach-back, does not close the readmission gap.

Formative Questions
Exercise 4.1 finds no significant association between language-concordant discharge and 30-day readmission. What is the most defensible interpretation?
Correct. Confounding by case complexity explains the null. More importantly, concordance addresses information delivery, not comprehension verification. Teach-back is the intervention that tests whether understanding occurred — and it produces the readmission effect that concordance alone cannot demonstrate.
Deprioritization based on a confounded null result would reduce a federally required and clinically necessary standard. The result tells you concordance is insufficient as a standalone intervention; it does not tell you it is unnecessary.
Sample size contributes, but the more important issue is confounding. Even a larger sample with the same confounding structure would be unlikely to isolate the concordance effect cleanly.
This interpretation would support reducing all discharge communication investments, which conflicts with the direct evidence from Hong et al. and Karliner et al. on teach-back and interpreter-assisted discharge outcomes.
