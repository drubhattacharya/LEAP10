Foundation 1 of 4 - The Synthesis
Seven modules. One argument. The financial case for language access is not a values argument. It is a measurement argument. You have now built the measurement.

Module 7 is the capstone. It does not introduce new evidence — it asks you to synthesize what you have learned across Modules 1 through 6 into a complete, financially defensible, leadership-ready argument. By this point you have the data, the causal mechanisms, the financial models, and the governance framework. The only remaining question is whether you can assemble them into a coherent case that produces a governance commitment — and whether that commitment is specific enough to be actionable.

What a Complete Argument Requires
A complete language access governance argument has four components: (1) the financial exposure — what the current gap costs, priced across the ROI dashboard tracks; (2) the clinical evidence — what the literature demonstrates about outcomes when access is provided; (3) the governance infrastructure — what the scorecard, accountability structure, and CAP commit the organization to; and (4) the investment case — what the proposed interventions cost and what they return. An argument missing any of these components can be deflected. An argument containing all four can be governed.

Foundation 2 of 4 - The Financial Architecture
The ROI Dashboard prices five distinct exposure tracks. Each has a different mechanism and a different audience.

The complete financial architecture connects each module to a specific ROI track. Understanding the mapping is required to present the financial case without conflating mechanisms — which is the most common error in executive language access arguments.

M1
Module 1 - ED Admission Avoidance (Tracks 1 and 2) and Stroke Sub-Model
Schulson 2018 OR 1.09 drives Track 1 (excess admission rate from diagnostic uncertainty at triage). 79.2% same-complaint return rate drives Track 2 (first-contact failure producing return visits). The stroke sub-model independently prices IV-tPA and EVT reimbursement forfeiture from symptom onset communication failure. Total ED exposure is the sum of Track 1 and Track 2; stroke is an illustration of the mechanism, not an additive track.
M3
Module 3 - Inpatient LOS Reduction (Lindholm anchor)
Lindholm 2012 0.75 to 1.47 additional days. Conservative model default: 1.0 day. Two-component: baseline interpreter integration at admission and discharge, plus QBS uplift (additional 0.5 days) for high-acuity service lines. Exercise 3.2 directionally consistent but underpowered — use Lindholm as the primary anchor.
M4
Module 4 - Readmission Avoidance (Hong HR 0.77)
Hong 2019 HR 0.77 applies to condition-related readmissions only. Apply to the LEP condition-related readmission subset, not total readmissions. Attribution percentage required. Dual adoption model: provider ramp (linear 12-month) plus automation S-curve. Automation ROI exceeds provider-only in back half of year 1. Do not add to LOS track — mechanisms are independent, not additive per encounter.
M5
Module 5 - Malpractice Defense Cost Avoidance (Berkeley $118K)
Berkeley 2010 $118,000 average defense cost, 91% incurred regardless of outcome. Two-track structure: Track 1 (negligence) + Track 2 (1557 civil rights) roughly doubles per-incident cost. Apply to estimated actual annual events (reported count divided by 0.256 for 74.4% underreporting correction). Do not overstate — conservative attribution is essential for CFO credibility.
M6
Module 6 - VBP and Audit Penalty Avoidance
HCAHPS communication domain scores drive VBP reimbursement. LEP HCAHPS gaps reduce scores that affect Medicare payments. The LEP HCAHPS gap is independently priced in the ROI Dashboard VBP module. Audit penalty avoidance prices the HHS OCR settlement risk from 1557 non-compliance — separate from malpractice defense costs and should not be double-counted with Module 5 tracks.

Foundation 3 of 4 - The CAP Architecture
A CAP without named owners, timelines, and accountability mechanisms is a document. A CAP with all three is a governance commitment.

Your Corrective Action Plan maps each module finding to a specific organizational intervention, assigns an owner, sets a 90-day and 12-month target, and specifies the governance mechanism that will confirm completion. The CAP template is pre-structured to capture all seven modules — your job is to enter your organization-specific numbers, owners, and timelines.

The Four CAP Elements
For each intervention in your CAP: (1) Finding — the specific data point or gap from the module exercises. (2) Intervention — the structural change, not the training or policy. (3) Owner — by name and title, not by department. (4) Accountability mechanism — the specific metric, reporting cadence, and escalation trigger if the target is not met. A CAP entry without an accountability mechanism is an aspiration, not a commitment.

The LAI score you entered at the beginning of the program is your baseline. The CAP targets are your 12-month improvement commitments. At the end of the program, re-enter your data into the LAI platform. The delta between your baseline and your 12-month score is the governance output of the CLEAR program — not the certificate, not the training completion, but the measurable improvement in the 20 KPIs that determine your organization readiness to deliver equitable care.

Foundation 4 of 4 - Gateway Check
Two synthesis questions before the final application begins.
Synthesis Questions
A CFO asks: "What is the total financial exposure from our language access gaps?" What is the correct answer structure?
Correct. Each track has a different mechanism, time horizon, and attribution logic. Summing them without qualification produces a number that a finance-literate CFO will immediately challenge. Presenting them separately with conservative anchors produces a more credible, if smaller, total that holds under scrutiny.
Summing all tracks without accounting for different mechanisms and time horizons produces a number that is inflated and difficult to defend. Track 1 (ED admissions) and Track 4 (malpractice defense) measure different things on different time horizons — they are not simply additive.
Presenting one track leaves the CFO without the full picture and misses the structural argument: multiple independent mechanisms produce language access financial exposure, making the investment case diversified and durable rather than dependent on a single pathway.
Deferring the financial answer until internal data is complete is appropriate for the final CAP calibration, but the CFO question requires a response now using conservative default assumptions. The ROI Dashboard defaults are specifically designed to be defensible before organization-specific data is entered.
What is the single most important characteristic that distinguishes a CAP that produces governance change from one that does not?
Correct. Organizations regularly produce comprehensive, financially precise, leadership-endorsed plans that are not implemented because no individual owns the execution and no mechanism triggers when targets are missed. Named accountability with an escalation trigger is what converts a plan into a governance commitment.
Comprehensiveness is valuable, but a comprehensive plan without named accountability produces comprehensive inaction. Coverage without ownership is the most common failure mode of institutional improvement plans.
Financial precision supports the investment decision but does not produce implementation. The ROI argument gets the plan approved; named accountability determines whether it gets executed.
Leadership approval creates organizational endorsement, which is important. But approval without named execution ownership and escalation mechanisms frequently produces plans that are endorsed but not implemented. Governance change requires the accountability structure, not just the endorsement.
