Foundation 1 of 5 · Design Principle
Interpreter delay is not a staffing problem. It is a workflow design problem.

Language access failure at triage rarely begins with a missing interpreter. It begins with a system that hasn't decided — in advance, in the workflow, in the EHR — exactly when and how interpreter access gets triggered. The result is predictable and consistent: delay concentrates in the service lines where the trigger is least reliable, not where interpreters are least available.

This distinction matters because the two diagnoses lead to completely different interventions. A staffing diagnosis produces more spending on interpreter contracts. A workflow design diagnosis produces a protocol change and an EHR field — both cheaper, both more durable, both testable.

Core Principle
The same organization can have adequate interpreter capacity and still produce clinically significant delay, because the trigger for using that capacity is inconsistent, late, or invisible in the workflow. Measuring delay time by service line is not a workforce metric — it is a process reliability metric.

The Kruskal-Wallis test compares median distributions across three or more groups without assuming normally distributed data — appropriate for delay times, which cluster differently by service line and don't follow a bell curve. An H-statistic of 57.0 at p < 0.0001 means the probability of observing this distribution pattern by chance is essentially zero. This is not a sampling artifact. It is a structural signal.[1]

Foundation 2 of 5 · Risk Gradient
Higher acuity and communication failure do not add. They multiply.

Exercise 1.2 tests whether advanced maternal LEP cases — patients with elevated clinical complexity — experience longer interpreter delay than standard triage encounters. The Mann-Whitney U test compares the two distributions: advanced maternal encounters versus core encounters.

The finding is clinically significant for a reason that goes beyond the delay numbers. Advanced maternal cases — preterm labor, obstetric emergencies, high-risk pregnancy — require faster interpreter access precisely because the clinical stakes are higher. The data shows the opposite is occurring: the patients who need the shortest delay are experiencing the longest.[2]

The Multiplicative Risk Pattern
A standard LEP patient experiencing a 23-minute delay faces communication risk. An advanced maternal LEP patient experiencing a 35-minute delay faces communication risk at the moment of highest clinical consequence. The risks don't add — they interact. A medication history misunderstood at triage becomes a contraindication missed during obstetric intervention. The delay is the same mechanism; the consequence is categorically different.

This is why service-line-specific interpreter protocols are not optional refinements — they are the minimum clinical governance response. A single hospital-wide delay target treats a 23-minute ED delay and a 35-minute OB delay as equivalent. They are not.

Foundation 3 of 5 · What Technology Cannot Do
Technology investment without workflow redesign does not remove the constraint.

Exercise 1.3 tests whether technology integration level is associated with reduced interpreter delay. The result is non-significant (p = 0.098). This is the most governance-relevant finding in Module 1 — not because it is surprising, but because it directly challenges a common institutional response to delay data.

How to Read a Non-Significant Result
"Non-significant" does not mean "no effect." It means the data does not provide sufficient evidence to confirm one. The p-value of 0.098 is close to the conventional threshold. What this result does confirm: in this dataset, technology integration level alone — without corresponding workflow redesign — does not reliably produce shorter delays. That is evidence about mechanism, not about technology value.

The mechanism explanation is straightforward. Technology reduces delay when it automates the trigger — when the EHR prompts an interpreter request at the right moment, routes it automatically, and timestamps the response. Technology does not reduce delay when it is available but not triggered — when the VRI cart exists in the hallway but the protocol for deploying it hasn't been written into the triage workflow.[3]

The governance implication: before approving any new technology investment in language access, ask explicitly what workflow step the technology is replacing or automating — and whether the trigger for that step is currently reliable. If the trigger isn't reliable, the technology will sit unused.

Foundation 4 of 5 · Financial Mechanism
Delay at triage activates three separate financial consequences — each with a different audience and a different mechanism.

The ROI Dashboard's ED module prices three distinct exposures. Understanding each mechanism — not just the dollar figure — is what makes the financial argument defensible when challenged by a CFO or CMO who pushes back on attribution.

T1
Track 1 — Admission Avoidance (all LEP ED encounters)
Schulson et al. (2018, n=57,435 ED visits): LEP patients significantly more likely to be admitted (OR 1.09) and to receive additional imaging (cardiovascular: OR 1.51). The mechanism is diagnostic uncertainty — when a clinician cannot obtain a complete history, they compensate with more tests and higher admission rates. Conservative model: 9% excess admission rate, 40% reduction target from timely access, 60% attribution to language access.[4]
T2
Track 2 — Same-Complaint Return Visit Avoidance
79.2% of LEP 48-hour ED return visits were for the same complaint — the direct signature of a first encounter where communication was insufficient to resolve the presenting problem. High same-complaint return rates are a leading indicator of systematic first-contact failure. Cost per event is modest (~$1,200 default) but volume signal is diagnostically important.
S
Stroke Sub-Model — High-Acuity Revenue Example
LEP patients cannot accurately communicate symptom onset time — the binary criterion for IV thrombolysis and EVT eligibility. The causal chain is precise and independently auditable by service line. The stroke sub-model is the most persuasive executive-facing example of the Track 1 mechanism. It demonstrates that the same delay dynamic that drives over-admission in Track 1 directly forecloses high-revenue interventions in stroke care.[5]

✓ Correct. Delay times cluster differently by service line and are right-skewed — OB outliers would distort an ANOVA mean comparison. Kruskal-Wallis compares rank distributions, making it robust to these patterns.
Not quite. ANOVA is not always inferior — it is preferred when data are normally distributed. The key issue here is that interpreter delay times are right-skewed, making Kruskal-Wallis the appropriate choice.
Sample size alone doesn't determine the test choice. The distribution shape matters more — delay times are right-skewed regardless of n.
H-statistic magnitude is a function of the actual distribution difference, not a property of the test that can be maximized by test selection.
